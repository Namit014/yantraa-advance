Here are the technical specifications, facts, numbers, and descriptions relevant to bomb disposal robots:

* The robots are not technically robots, but rather drones operated by a human operator from a safe distance.
* They are used to safely disable explosive ordnance, including bombs, landmines, and unexploded munitions.
* The first bomb disposal robot created was the Wheelbarrow Mark 1 in 1972.
* The original bomb disposal robots were controlled by a series of ropes.
* Later, a telecommunications cable was used to transmit commands to the robot's electrical systems.
* Today, the majority of bomb disposal robots are controlled through wireless communications, with a range of a couple of kilometers.
* The design of bomb disposal robots has changed little since they were first created, with the core idea remaining the same.
* Bomb disposal robots typically achieve their purpose by firing a high-pressure jet of water at wires on the device.
* The key to modern bomb disposal operations is to render the explosive ordnance device inert without causing it to detonate.
* The bomb disposal robot's arm allows great versatility and can be used to bypass various obstacles that would otherwise impede its progress.
* Some designs can even climb stairs.
* The cost of bomb disposal robots goes into making the electronics and sensors robust in really brutal conditions, like space.
* Some of the newer models can even climb stairs.
* Bomb disposal robots vary in size, from the stripped-down backpack-sized robots that can be carried on a soldier's back and thrown into buildings, to the size of a ride-on lawnmower, armed with x-ray devices and explosives detectors.
* Initially, the controls for these robots were complicated, requiring specific training; now games console controllers are being used to operate them.
* The prerequisite, in such high-pressure environments, is to make the controls as intuitive and as simple as possible.
* Prototypes are being developed that are able to jump over walls and land on the other side.
* Others are being developed that have two arms, providing bomb disposal robots with greater manual dexterity, such as allowing them to open car boots and look inside.
