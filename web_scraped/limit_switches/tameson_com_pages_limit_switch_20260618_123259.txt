A **limit switch** is an electromechanical device that detects the presence, absence, or travel limit of an object by **physical contact** and converts that movement into an electrical signal.[9][11] It is commonly used in industrial control and safety applications to stop, start, count, or monitor machinery and moving parts.[9][11]

- **How it works:** an actuator is mechanically linked to internal contacts; when an object presses the actuator, the contacts open or close depending on the switch configuration.[1][2][4]
- **Typical functions:** end-of-travel detection, position sensing, safety interlocking, and object counting.[2][5][8][11]
- **Common applications:** conveyors, elevators, machine tools, refrigerator doors, garage doors, and hydraulic or pneumatic systems.[2][5][6][8]
- **Construction:** basic parts include the actuator, switch body/contact block, and terminals; the enclosure protects internal parts from water, oil, dust, gas, and mechanical damage.[3][6]
- **Contact types:** **normally open (NO)** and **normally closed (NC)** are the main configurations described in the sources.[1][2]
- **Materials:** stainless steel is used where moisture or corrosion resistance is needed; in chemically harsh environments, copper contacts may be chosen over silver for better corrosion resistance in some cases.[10]
- **Micro limit switches:** these are smaller versions for tight spaces, with small actuating plungers and typical ratings around **250 VAC** and **10–15 A**.[10]
- **Advantages:** rugged, low power, easy to install, and capable of directly switching higher currents without an added relay in many cases.[10]
- **Disadvantages:** they are mechanical, so they are better suited to lower speeds, require direct contact, and can wear out over time.[10]

The core principle is the same across sources: a physical motion is translated into an electrical state change that a controller or circuit can use.[1][2][4][9]
