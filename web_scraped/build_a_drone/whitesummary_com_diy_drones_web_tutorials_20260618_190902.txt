# DIY Drones: 5 Internet Tutorials Testing Your Acumen

The web is filled with DIY drone tutorials that vary widely in technical depth, financial scope, and target audience. Even beginners in mechanics can build the simplest drones, though most tutorials assume at least a modicum of experience in assembling components into a functional device.

## 1. DIY Drones for Beginners

This tutorial emphasizes the obstacles encountered when moving from theory to practice. It explains terminology needed to evaluate kits, components, and tools. It recommends starting with a simple wooden-frame quadcopter—a four-arm device powered by four electrical motors, one per arm. A tricopter (three motors) is cheaper but more difficult to balance in terms of power output.

Key components include a Li-Po battery, four brushless DC electric motors (ECMs), a flight controller unit, an electronic speed controller (ESC), and video equipment. The critical challenge is balancing power output and harmonizing the electronic components. After attaching propellers and prop guards, a test flight should follow—though the first flight is almost always a failure. The tutorial includes both a downloadable guide and a 1+ hour video, with the video being more informative.

## 2. A Six-Part Guide to Building Your First Drone

More succinct than the first tutorial, this guide includes relevant pictures for the first three steps covering the drone's infrastructure. It names specific brand-name parts, making it easier to search for components online. Steps 4 through 6 use video demonstrations, which are particularly helpful for explaining mechanical processes.

## 3. The 1-Hour Quadcopter

Despite the title, the authors acknowledge that even experienced builders would need at least a day. The tutorial features highly detailed sketches, pertinent images, and cost-cutting tips. It includes frequent warnings about potentially dangerous actions, making it a useful resource for beginners.

## 4. Seven Lessons in UAV (and) Mechanics

This scholarly guide includes information that extends beyond drone building into general physics. It uses higher-quality materials and parts than basic DIY kits, aiming to produce a more sophisticated result. While informative, it may be more of a learning exercise than a practical build guide for casual hobbyists.

## 5. The $3,500 Experiment

Published at airspacemag.com, this account follows a former war reporter attempting to build military-grade drones. It uses premium components: a carbon fiber frame, Y6 configuration, complicated wiring systems, and a minuscule control module. It is not a one-man project and functions more as storytelling than a practical guide. The test flight ended in complete failure.
