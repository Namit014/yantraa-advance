A **3D depth camera** captures not just color or brightness, but a **depth map** where each pixel represents distance to the camera.[2][12] The main ways it does this are **stereo vision**, **structured light**, and **time-of-flight (ToF)**, which all estimate depth using different physical principles.[3][4][13]

**How depth is measured**
- **Triangulation**: stereo vision and structured light infer depth from geometric shift between viewpoints or from deformation of a projected pattern.[3][4][13]
- **Time-of-flight**: ToF measures how long light takes to leave the camera, hit an object, and return; distance is derived from that travel time.[2][3][4]

**Stereo vision**
- Uses two cameras separated by a fixed **baseline** to capture slightly offset views of the same scene.[3][7][13]
- The offset between matching points is called **disparity**; closer objects produce higher disparity, farther objects lower disparity.[3][5][13]
- The method is most effective when the scene has enough texture for correspondence matching.[3][5]

**Passive vs. active stereo**
- **Passive stereo** depends on ambient light and scene texture, so it works well in bright environments but can fail in low light or on textureless surfaces.[3][5]
- **Active stereo** adds an IR projector to create artificial texture, improving performance in low light and on bland surfaces.[3][5]

**Structured light**
- Projects a **known pattern** onto the scene and analyzes how the pattern deforms on surfaces to compute depth via triangulation.[3][4][9]
- It is especially strong at **close to medium range** with high accuracy and resolution.[2]
- It can struggle on **specular or mirror-like surfaces**, in **shadows**, and in **strong sunlight** when the projected pattern is washed out.[3][5]

**Time-of-flight (ToF)**
- Uses modulated infrared illumination and measures either the direct return time or the phase shift of the reflected light.[3][5][7]
- **Direct ToF (dToF)** measures photon travel time more directly and can be very accurate at longer ranges.[5]
- **Indirect ToF (iToF)** is more common in depth cameras and computes distance from phase differences.[5]
- ToF works in darkness and has low processing overhead because depth is produced directly by the sensor.[5]

**Why depth cameras matter**
- They enable machines to understand **scene geometry**, **object distance**, and **spatial relationships** in real time.[1][8][12]
- Common applications include **robot navigation**, **grasping/pick-and-place**, **AR/VR**, **gesture recognition**, and **3D reconstruction**.[1][7][10][12]
