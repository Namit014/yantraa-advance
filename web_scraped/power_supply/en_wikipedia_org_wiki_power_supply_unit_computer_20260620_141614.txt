============================================================

Power supply units (PSUs) convert mains AC to low-voltage regulated DC power for internal computer components. Modern personal computers universally use switched-mode power supplies. Some PSUs have a manual input voltage selector, while others adapt automatically. Most modern desktop personal computer PSUs conform to the ATX specification, which includes form factor and voltage tolerances. While connected to the mains, an ATX PSU always provides a 5-volt standby (5VSB) power. ATX PSUs are turned on and off by a signal from the motherboard and also provide a "Power Good" signal to the motherboard indicating when DC voltages are within specification. The most recent ATX PSU standard is version 3.1 as of mid 2025.

PSUs convert AC from a wall socket to low-voltage DC for the motherboard, processor, and peripherals. They supply multiple DC voltages, referred to as voltage rails, which must be regulated for stable operation. Some PSUs also supply a standby voltage to allow for system power-off states, remote startup via wake-on-LAN, wake-on-ring, or local startup via Keyboard Power ON (KBPO) if supported by the motherboard.

Early microcomputer and home computer PSUs used heavy step-down transformers and linear power supplies. The Apple II, introduced in 1977, utilized a switched-mode power supply (SMPS), which was lighter, smaller, and fanless compared to linear supplies. SMPS use high-frequency transformers and power transistors that switch thousands of times per second, allowing for precise output voltage control without dissipating energy as heat in a linear regulator. The development of economical high-power and high-voltage transistors made SMPS practical for desktop PCs. Modern computers exclusively use SMPS for their lighter weight, lower cost, and higher efficiency. Computer PSUs may incorporate protection against short circuits, overloads, over-voltage, under-voltage, over-current, and over-temperature.

Some PSUs designed for worldwide use featured an input voltage selector. In lower voltage ranges (around 115 V), this switch could reconfigure the rectifier into a voltage doubler. Higher voltage ranges (around 230 V) required different configurations of filter capacitors and protective components. Power-factor correction (PFC) systems evolved from passive PFC, which used higher-capacity filter capacitors and an inductor, to active PFC. Active PFC achieves higher power factors (up to 0.99 or higher) and often uses a step-up converter to supply a single 400 V filter capacitor from a wide input voltage range (typically 80-240 V). Active PFC circuits also replaced older inrush current limiters.

The first IBM PC power supply unit (PSU) supplied +5 V and +12 V, along with limited amounts of -5 V and -12 V. The +5 V rail delivered most of the 63.5 W capacity, primarily powering microchips, while the +12 V rail powered motors in disk drives and cooling fans. The -12 V rail provided negative voltage for RS-232 serial ports, and the -5 V rail was for ISA bus peripherals, though not widely used by motherboards beyond the original IBM PC. An additional "Power Good" wire prevented digital circuitry operation during initial power-on milliseconds until stable voltages were achieved. Original IBM PC, XT, and AT PSUs had a line-voltage power switch on the case. These early PSUs were typically always on, lacking power-saving modes like standby or soft-off. In case of a short circuit, they would either blow a fuse or, if an SMPS, repeatedly attempt to restart.

The ATX standard, published by Intel in 1995, was developed as microchips operating on 3.3 V became more prevalent.
