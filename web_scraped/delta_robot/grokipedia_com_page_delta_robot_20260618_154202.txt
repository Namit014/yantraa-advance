The Delta robot is a parallel manipulator with three articulated arms that each connect a fixed base via universal joints to a mobile platform through spherical joints, providing three translational degrees of freedom (X, Y, Z) for high‑speed, precise motion of lightweight objects (typically ≤ 10 kg). Its parallelogram linkages keep the end‑effector orientation constant, enabling accelerations exceeding 15 g and operation rates above 200 cycles per minute in modern variants, with repeatability better than 0.1 mm. The robot’s structure uses upper arms designed for torque transmission and lower arms made from lightweight materials such as carbon‑fiber composites to reduce inertia. Actuation is performed by stationary base‑mounted rotary servo motors that drive the upper arms; only the lightweight linkages and platform move, which minimizes moving‑mass inertia and allows rapid motion. The operational workspace is a dome‑ or cylinder‑shaped volume beneath the base, limited by arm lengths, joint angles, and the geometry of the parallelograms. In its standard form the robot offers three degrees of freedom; an optional fourth degree of freedom for rotation can be added via an inner leg with prismatic and spherical joints. Since its invention by Swiss engineer Reymond Clavel at EPFL in 1985 (prototype 1985, commercial license to Demaurex in 1987), the Delta robot has been commercialized since the early 1990s, becoming a standard in food processing, pharmaceuticals, electronics assembly, watchmaking, and tele‑surgery. Its compact footprint, low maintenance, and compatibility with standard servo motors and controllers have led to thousands of units deployed worldwide. Clavel’s work earned the 1999 Golden Robot Award from ABB Flexible Automation.
