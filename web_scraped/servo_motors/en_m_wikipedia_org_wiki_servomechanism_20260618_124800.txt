Servomechanism - Technical Specifications and Facts:

**Definition**: A servomechanism (or servo) is a control system managing the position and time derivatives (e.g., velocity) of a mechanical system. It employs closed-loop control with error-sensing negative feedback to minimize steady-state error and enhance dynamic response. The term "servo" derives from the Latin "servus" (slave) and applies to systems where feedback corrects mechanical position, speed, or measurable variables.

**Components**:  
- **Feedback Mechanisms**: Encoders (digital) or potentiometers (analog) measure position. Encoders also provide speed feedback, enabling precise control via PID controllers.  
- **Actuation**: Typically uses servomotors (electric, hydraulic, or pneumatic). Industrial servomotors may include brush-type DC motors, planetary gearsets, and optical encoders.  
- **Control**: H-bridge controllers drive motors to reduce error signals. Pulse-width modulation (PWM) signals are used in RC servos to command position.

**Types**:  
- **Industrial Servomotors**: High-precision components using rotary encoders and PID controllers.  
- **RC Servos**: Low-cost, use free-running motors and potentiometers. Stepper motors are distinct (open-loop, no feedback) and used in medium-precision applications.  

**Applications**:  
- Position Control: Globe control valves, automotive power windows (non-servo without feedback), cruise control (closed-loop servo).  
- Speed Control: Steam engine governors, constant-speed aircraft propellers, gas turbine fuel controls.  
- Others: Fire-control systems, satellite-tracking antennas, fly-by-wire aircraft, autofocus cameras, hard disk drives (sub-micrometer accuracy), and robotics.  

**History**:  
- Early Uses: Ship steering systems (1866, SS Great Eastern), James Watt's steam engine governor (mechanical feedback).  
- Innovations: Telemotor (1872, Andrew Betts Brown) simplified remote mechanisms. Ragonnet (1909) air/steam-powered servo amplifier.  
- Electrical Development: Elisha Gray's Telautograph (1888). WWII-era electrical fire-control systems with amplidyne amplifiers. UNISERVO tape drive for UNIVAC I used vacuum tube amplifiers.  

**Key Notes**:  
- Position-controlled servos often include built-in encoders or feedback mechanisms.  
- Servomotors differ from stepper motors due to closed-loop feedback.  
- Temperature affects potentiometers, whereas encoders offer greater stability.
