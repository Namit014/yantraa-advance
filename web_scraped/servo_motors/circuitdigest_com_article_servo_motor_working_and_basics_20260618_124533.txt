A servo motor is a precision actuator that operates on closed‑loop feedback control, enabling it to rotate to specific angles with high accuracy. Common hobby models include the micro SG90 and the metal‑gear MG995, while industrial servos are supplied by manufacturers such as Siemens, Yaskawa and Fanuc. Torque is expressed in kilogram‑centimetres (kg/cm); typical hobby servos are rated at 3 kg/cm, 6 kg/cm or 12 kg/cm, indicating the weight the motor can lift when the load is positioned 1 cm from the shaft. Servo motors are widely used in robotics, Arduino‑based projects, RC vehicles and home‑automation systems.

The control system of a servo consists of four essential elements: a control circuit that processes input signals, a position sensor (usually a potentiometer) providing real‑time shaft‑position feedback, a DC or AC motor that produces motion, and a gear assembly that reduces speed while increasing torque. The system continuously compares the desired position (input PWM signal) with the actual position (potentiometer feedback). Any difference generates an error signal that drives the motor until the feedback matches the command, at which point the motor stops.

Servo motors are controlled via PWM (pulse‑width modulation) with a standard pulse period of 20 ms (50 Hz). Pulse width determines the angle: 1 ms corresponds to 0°, 1.5 ms to the neutral 90° position, and 2 ms to 180°. Hobby servos typically accept a supply voltage of 4.8 V–6 V and can rotate through a 0°–180° range. Interfacing with a microcontroller requires three wires: two for power (positive and ground) and one for the PWM signal. The motor can be driven directly from a 5 V rail, but a servo shield is recommended when more than two servos are used to manage current draw.

Advanced servo drives support simultaneous control of position, velocity and torque. Libraries such as Arduino’s Servo.h simplify programming by allowing angular positions to be set in degrees. Industrial servo drive units offer multi‑axis coordination (up to 64 axes), high‑resolution absolute encoders (20‑24 bit), functional safety to SIL3/PLe levels, communication over EtherCAT, PROFINET or Modbus, and energy‑saving features like regenerative braking. A standard servo can move approximately ±90° from its neutral position, giving a total sweep of about 180°.
