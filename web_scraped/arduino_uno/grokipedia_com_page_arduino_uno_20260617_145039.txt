============================================================

The Arduino Uno is a microcontroller board based on the ATmega328P (classic versions) or ATmega4809 (Uno R4 iterations), featuring 14 digital I/O pins (6 PWM-capable), 6 analog inputs, and a 16 MHz quartz crystal oscillator in original models. It operates at 5V with a 40 mA per-pin I/O limit and 6 PWM frequencies ranging from 490 Hz (pins 5/6) to 977 Hz (pins 9–13). Power options include USB (5V) or an external DC source (7–12V recommended; 6–20V accepted), with a 2A current limit via USB. Physical dimensions are 68.6×53.4 mm, matching the Arduino form factor for shield compatibility. The board includes 32 KB flash memory, 2 KB SRAM, and 1 KB EEPROM in 8-bit variants, while Uno R4 boards use a 32-bit ARM Cortex-M0+ chip with 256 KB flash. Designed for simplicity and standardization, it integrates a micro-USB port for programming/USB scanning, ICSP header, and a reset button. First released in 2010 as the Uno, it evolved from Arduino's earlier Serial and Diecimila revisions. Sold over 10 million units by 2021, it remains popular in education and hobbyist projects. A 2023 Uno R4 revision introduced USB-C and a 32-bit processor, with a 2025 Qualcomm-acquired Uno Q version using a Snapdragon-based chip. The Arduino IDE supports C/C++ programming with simplified syntax, and the ecosystem features extensive shields, libraries, and community-driven extensions under Creative Commons licensing.
