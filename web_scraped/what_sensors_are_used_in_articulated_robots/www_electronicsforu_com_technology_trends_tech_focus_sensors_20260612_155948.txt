Biosensors employ living organisms or biological molecules such as enzymes or antibodies to detect the presence of chemicals; examples include blood glucose monitors, electronic noses and DNA biosensors. Raindrop sensors sense rain and weather conditions and convert the sensed signal into a reference number. For machine perception, AI robots rely on sensory inputs such as cameras, microphones, wireless signals, active lidar, sonar and radar to infer facial, object and gesture details. A micro‑robot intended for biological environments can integrate sensors, signal‑processing circuitry, a memory unit and a feedback loop, all operating at a micro‑scale, enabling integrated micro‑bio‑robotic systems for biological engineering research. A typical robot consists of a movable structure driven by motors, sensor circuitry, a power supply and a controlling computer. Actuators may be electric motors and solenoids, hydraulic systems or pneumatic systems. Autonomous robots use their sensors to perform tasks without human intervention. Notable robots mentioned include Honda’s ASIMO (two‑legged walking), educational robotic arms or wheeled platforms, industrial assembly‑line robots, BattleBots (remote‑controlled fighters), DRDO’s Daksh (battery‑operated, remote‑controlled wheeled robot for bomb recovery), Robomow (lawn‑mowing robot), MindStorms (Lego‑based programmable robots), QRIO and RoboSapien (voice recognition and walking), and the sensor‑driven robotic arm used in the film Gravity to carry cameras, lights, props and actors.
