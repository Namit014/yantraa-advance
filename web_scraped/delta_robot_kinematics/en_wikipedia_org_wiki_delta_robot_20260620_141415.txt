============================================================

A **delta robot** is a type of **parallel robot** with three arms connected to universal joints at the base, and its parallelogram-link arm design keeps the end effector’s orientation fixed.[1] It was invented in the early 1980s by a team led by **Reymond Clavel** at **EPFL in Switzerland** and was originally developed to handle small, light objects very quickly, including pralines for packaging.[1]

Key technical facts:

- The delta robot is a **parallel robot**, meaning multiple kinematic chains connect the base to the end effector.[1]
- Its arms use **parallelograms**, which constrain the end platform to **pure translation** in \(X\), \(Y\), and \(Z\) with no rotation in the standard 3-DOF form.[1]
- The actuators are mounted on the **base**, so the moving parts have **low inertia**, enabling very high speed and acceleration.[1]
- A common use is **high-speed pick-and-place** in factories; some systems can reach **up to 300 picks per minute**.[1][3]
- The original Clavel version had **4 degrees of freedom**: three translations plus one rotation about the vertical axis.[1]
- Later variants include **6-DOF**, **4-DOF**, **direct-drive**, and **linear-delta** designs, as well as compact micro-scale versions.[1]
- Delta robots are used in **packaging**, **food handling**, **electronics assembly**, **cleanroom applications**, **medical positioning**, **haptics**, and **delta-style 3D printers**.[1]

Historical details:

- In **1987**, the Swiss company **Demaurex** licensed the delta robot for packaging applications.[1]
- In **1999**, **ABB** began selling the **FlexPicker** delta robot.[1]
- By the end of **1999**, **Sigpack Systems** was also selling delta robots.[1]

Performance and miniaturization:

- A 2017 Harvard microrobotics version was reduced to **0.43 g** and a size of **15 mm × 15 mm × 20 mm**.[1]
- That miniature robot could move a **1.3 g payload** in a **7 cubic millimeter workspace** with **5 micrometers precision**.[1]
- It reached speeds of **0.45 m/s**, accelerations of **215 m/s²**, and repetitive motion at **75 Hz**.[1]
