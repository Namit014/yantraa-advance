**Actuator Types:**

1. Mechanical: uses motion or forces of other parts of the system
2. Hydraulic: uses pressure of a liquid (usually oil) to cause a piston to slide
3. Pneumatic: uses a gas (usually air) instead of a liquid
4. Electric: uses an electric motor to generate torque
5. Electromechanical: uses mechanical means to convert rotational force into linear motion
6. Electrohydraulic: uses electric motor to provide torque to operate a hydraulic accumulator
7. Rotary: uses a rotary motor to turn the target part over a certain angle
8. Linear: uses a linear motor to produce a linear force along their length

**Actuator Characteristics:**

1. Displacement: linear, rotary, or oscillatory motion
2. Torque: generated by electric, hydraulic, or pneumatic means
3. Control signal: low-energy input signal (voltage, electric current, pneumatic, or hydraulic fluid pressure)
4. Source of energy: electric, hydraulic, pneumatic, or manual
5. Feedback mechanism: may use sensors or feedback loops to monitor and control performance

**Actuator Applications:**

1. Machine control systems
2. Valve actuation
3. Industrial fluid transport installations
4. Health care devices
5. Factory automation
6. Excavation equipment
7. Construction equipment
8. Motion control systems

**Actuator Specifications:**

1. Speed: range of 0-100,000 RPM (rotary), 0-100 km/h (linear)
2. Torque: 100 kN (electromechanical), 5-30 kN (linear motor)
3. Acceleration: limited (hydraulic actuator), high (electromechanical actuator)
4. Durability: high (electromechanical actuator), relatively high (pneumatic actuator)
5. Maintenance: low (electromechanical actuator), high (pneumatic actuator)

**Actuator Categories:**

1. Incremental-drive actuators (stepper motors)
2. Continuous-drive actuators (DC torque motors, induction motors, hydraulic motors, pneumatic motors, piston-cylinder drives)
