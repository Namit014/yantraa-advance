Industrial robotic arms are **programmable mechanical manipulators** that mimic human arm motion to perform repetitive tasks with high precision and repeatability.[4][9] They typically use interconnected links and joints, an end-effector such as a gripper or welding torch, and a controller that executes programmed movements.[4][2]

**Main types of industrial robot arms**
- **Articulated arms**: the most common type, usually with 4–6 axes and the greatest flexibility for welding, painting, assembly, and material handling.[1][4]
- **SCARA arms**: designed for high-speed horizontal movements, compact footprints, and applications like pick-and-place, assembly, and packaging.[8]
- **Delta arms**: parallel-link robots built for very fast motion and short cycle times, often used in food processing, pharmaceutical packaging, and sorting.[8]
- **Cartesian (gantry) arms**: move along linear X, Y, and Z axes and are suited to large work envelopes, heavy payloads, and precise linear movement.[8]
- **Collaborative robots (cobots)**: designed for safer human-robot collaboration and often used without traditional caging in shared workspaces.[8][2]

**Key technical characteristics**
- **Degrees of freedom (DOF)** determine how many independent movements the arm can make; more DOF increases flexibility and range of motion.[1][2]
- Industrial arms commonly operate with **4 to 8 axes** depending on the application.[1][3]
- Selection criteria include **payload**, **reach**, **repeatability**, **speed/cycle time**, **accuracy**, and **working environment**.[5][12][13]
- Repeatability is often more important than absolute accuracy in industrial use.[5][13]

**Common applications**
- **Manufacturing**: assembly, welding, fastening, painting, machining, and cutting.[4][7][12]
- **Logistics**: palletizing, sorting, packaging, and material handling.[4][12]
- **Quality control**: inspection, testing, and verification.[4][13]
- **Electronics and food/pharma**: high-speed assembly, pick-and-place, and hygienic packaging tasks.[8][12]

**Performance and benefits**
- Robotic arms can operate **continuously** without fatigue and maintain consistent output over long periods.[4][8]
- They provide **high precision**, **repeatability**, improved throughput, and reduced labor costs and defect rates.[7][12]
- Some industrial robotic systems are reported to deliver **sub-millimeter accuracy** in precision tasks.[8]

**Integration with autonomous mobile robots**
- Combining robotic arms with **autonomous mobile robots (AMRs)** creates flexible, reconfigurable automation for multi-location tasks and dynamic workflows.[8]
- This approach reduces dependence on fixed infrastructure and is useful when production needs change frequently.[8]

**How to choose the right arm**
- Match the arm’s **payload capacity** to the maximum expected load, typically with a margin of **20–30%**.[8]
- Verify the arm’s **reach** covers the full 3D workspace.[8][12]
- Balance **speed** against precision needs.[8]
- Evaluate **total cost of ownership**, including installation, training, and maintenance.[8]
- Check whether the application needs simple position control or more advanced sensing and adaptive behavior.[10][2]
