The five types highlighted in the source are **articulated**, **SCARA**, **delta**, **cartesian/gantry**, and **collaborative robots (cobots)**.[3] These robot arms are chosen based on motion pattern, payload, speed, precision, and workspace constraints.[1][3]

- **Articulated robot arms** resemble a human arm and use multiple rotary joints; they are the most common industrial type and are used for welding, painting, assembly, and heavy material handling.[1][2][3]
- **SCARA robots** are designed for fast horizontal motion and are especially suited to pick-and-place work in electronics and packaging.[3][11][12]
- **Delta robot arms** are lightweight, high-speed parallel robots used for fast handling of small items in packaging, food processing, and pharmaceuticals.[1][3][11]
- **Cartesian/Gantry robots** move in straight lines along the X, Y, and Z axes and are used for tasks such as 3D printing, palletizing, and material handling.[1][3][11]
- **Collaborative robots (cobots)** are built to work safely alongside humans and are used in tasks like quality inspection, assembly, and packaging.[1][3]

The broader industrial-robot classification used by the International Federation of Robotics also includes **cylindrical robots**, while some sources list **polar/spherical** and other variants as well.[1][11] Articulated robots are commonly defined as having **5 or more joints**, and six-axis robots are a common articulated subtype with **six degrees of freedom**.[1][5][10]

For the industrial applications described in the source, the main matches are:

- **Automotive:** articulated and six-axis robots for welding, painting, and assembly.[3][5][9]
- **Electronics:** SCARA and delta robots for precise pick-and-place of small components.[3][9][11]
- **Food and pharma:** delta robots for high-speed sorting, packaging, and inspection.[3][11]
- **Human-robot collaboration:** cobots for assisted assembly, inspection, and packaging.[1][3]

The source also notes that robot selection depends on **task type**, **payload**, **reach**, **accuracy**, **speed**, **available space**, and **integration/maintenance needs**.[3]
